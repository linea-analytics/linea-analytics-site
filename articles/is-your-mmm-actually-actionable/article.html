Actionable MMM Outputs

Many MMM projects still fall short where it matters most: driving decisions. The issue starts with the model itself, which then affects the outputs.

Non-actionable MMM: what it looks like, and why it fails

Most MMM outputs look impressive at first glance. Clean charts, channel contributions, perhaps even some response curves. The problem is that they often stop there.

If you are paying thousands of pounds every year, and your decisions are not affected by measurement, you should take a closer look at how your measurement is being done.

As with any piece of analysis in business, the overarching criteria for success are:

  • Is it correctly answering your questions?
  • Is it providing recommendations that are specific enough for you to take action?

While these might be obvious requirements for an MMM project, unfortunately that is not always the case. This is especially true today where automated solutions are more widely available because they are easy to set up. This does not mean that they are worth the cost, even when cheap or free! I. Outputs that actually guide decisions

The most actionable output in MMM is optimisation.

Optimisation translates model learnings into decisions: how much to spend, where, and when. Without it, MMM remains descriptive and backwards-looking.

However, many optimisation outputs are still too high level. A common example is total budget allocation across channels. While useful directionally, this is too vague for real planning.

Media is not deployed as a yearly lump sum. It is executed weekly, sometimes daily. Missing the optimal timing of spend can be just as costly as misallocating the total budget.

The solution: optimised laydown

What’s needed is a time-phased plan.

An optimised weekly laydown bridges the gap between strategy and execution. It tells you not just how much to spend, but when to spend it. This captures seasonality, diminishing returns, and timing effects that static allocations ignore.

In practice, this is often visualised at a monthly level for readability, but the underlying logic should operate at a finer granularity.

II. From static optimisation to scenarios

Optimisation tools are now a baseline expectation. If an MMM output does not include one, it is incomplete, please reach out if that is the case!

But the best tools go further. They move from static recommendations to dynamic scenario planning.

Forward-looking decisions are rarely made under fixed conditions. Budgets change, targets shift, markets evolve. An optimisation that only works for one set of assumptions has limited value.

A robust scenario tool should allow flexibility across several dimensions:

  • Objective
    Optimise for a fixed budget, or determine the budget required to hit a revenue target.
  • Time horizon
    Plan for the next 1, 3, 6, or 12 months depending on business needs.
  • Channel controls
    • Include or exclude specific channels (e.g. only performance media)
    • Apply minimum and maximum spend constraints
    • Introduce new or hypothetical channels with custom response curves
  • Market conditions and synergies
    Media does not operate in isolation. A strong model captures how channels interact with each other and with external factors such as demand or competition. Visit this page for more information.

Incorporating these synergies into optimisation allows planning for realistic scenarios: competitor pressure increasing, demand softening, or channels amplifying each other.

III. Granularity: where actionability really comes from

Granularity is one of the most overlooked drivers of actionability.

Annual, channel-level results are about as far removed from execution as possible. They may inform high-level strategy, but they do not guide day-to-day decisions.

Modern MMM frameworks should go much deeper:

  • Channel → sub-channel → placement level
  • Regional splits
  • Product-level models

The closer the output is to the level at which decisions are made, the more actionable it becomes.

For example, knowing that paid social is saturated is less useful than knowing which campaign type, in which region, is hitting diminishing returns. That is the level at which planners can act.

Combining multiple layers of granularity creates a system where insights are not just descriptive, but operational.

IV. Using MMM to improve measurement itself

Actionable MMM does not stop at media planning. It should also inform how you measure going forward.

One of the biggest challenges in MMM is collinearity, where channels move together and make it difficult to isolate effects. This is often a data problem, not just a modelling problem.

MMM outputs can highlight where variation is lacking. For instance, if two channels are always activated together, their individual contributions will remain uncertain.

This creates an opportunity: use MMM to design better experiments.

By deliberately introducing variation in media activity, you improve the model’s ability to learn. Over time, this leads to more reliable insights and better optimisation.

Closing thought

The value of MMM is not in explaining the past, but in shaping the future.

Actionable outputs share three traits:

  • They translate directly into decisions
  • They operate at the level where execution happens
  • They adapt to changing conditions

Anything less is just reporting.
